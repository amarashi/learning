Glossary

The vocabulary of the course. These definitions are the ones the lessons stick to. Print it; keep it beside you. Grows as we go.

The big distinction

Concept Graphic design

Arranging visual material to communicate a message — to inform, organise, or persuade. Not decoration; decision-making about what the viewer sees first, second, third, and how it all hangs together.

Elements The elements of design

The raw materials — the "nouns" you place on a page. The set you'll see most: line, shape, form, space, value, colour, texture, and type. On their own they carry no meaning; design is what you do with them.

Principles The principles of design

The rules for arranging elements — the "grammar." They decide relationships, emphasis and order: contrast, repetition, alignment, proximity, hierarchy, balance, white space (and the Gestalt grouping rules). Principles are verbs you apply to the element nouns.

The elements (raw materials)

Element Line

A mark connecting two points; also any edge the eye follows. Divides, connects, points, and implies direction or movement.

Element Shape

A flat, bounded area (circle, square, blob). The 2-D silhouette of a thing before light, depth or surface are added.

Element Form

Shape with the illusion of three dimensions — volume implied by light, shadow, or perspective. Shape + depth.

Element Space

The emptiness around and between things. Negative space / white space is the unused area — and it is a deliberate design material, not leftover.

Element Value

How light or dark something is, independent of its colour. The most powerful element for creating contrast and readable hierarchy. (Squint test: value is what survives.)

Element Colour

The hue dimension on top of value. Described by hue (which colour), saturation (how intense), and value/lightness (how light/dark).

Element Texture

The implied or real surface quality — rough, smooth, grainy, glossy. Adds richness and tactility.

Element Type

Letters as a visual element — their shapes, sizes, weights and spacing. Type is read and seen; it carries tone as well as words.

The principles (the C.R.A.P. core)

Popularised by Robin Williams, The Non-Designer's Design Book.

Principle Contrast

Making things that are different look clearly different (big vs small, dark vs light, bold vs thin). Creates emphasis and guides the eye. Rule of thumb: if two things aren't the same, make them very different — not slightly.

Spot it A headline much larger and bolder than its body text.

Principle Repetition

Repeating visual choices (a colour, font, spacing, shape) across a piece to make it feel like one coherent whole and to build rhythm. The seed of consistency and, later, brand.

Spot it Every heading in the same colour and weight throughout.

Principle Alignment

Lining elements up to a shared edge or centre so nothing is placed arbitrarily. Creates invisible lines that make a layout feel organised and intentional. Strong rule: pick one alignment and commit.

Spot it All text sharing one left edge down the page.

Principle Proximity

Placing related items close together and unrelated items apart, so groups read as groups. Distance encodes relationship — the fastest way to make a mess look organised.

Spot it A caption tucked under its image, away from the next one.

The principles (beyond the core)

Principle Hierarchy (visual hierarchy)

Deliberately ordering elements by importance so the eye knows what to read first, second, third. Built mostly out of contrast, size, value, and proximity. The single biggest difference between "designed" and "typed."

Principle Balance

The distribution of visual weight across a layout. Symmetrical (mirrored, formal) or asymmetrical (uneven but counter-weighted, dynamic).

Principle White space (negative space)

Intentional emptiness used to separate, group, and let elements breathe. Often the difference between "cheap" and "premium." Not wasted space.

Concept Gestalt principles

Rules describing how the eye automatically groups what it sees — by proximity, similarity, closure, common region, and figure/ground. They explain why the arranging principles work.
