Lesson 02 · The C.R.A.P. core — A & P

Proximity & Alignment — instant tidiness

The two cheapest principles in design. They add no colour, no font, no content — they just decide where things sit. And they do more than anything else to turn "amateur" into "organised."

Of the four C.R.A.P. principles, these two are about space and edges. Master them first: they're nearly free, almost mechanical, and they fix the most common reason a layout feels messy — things placed without a reason.

1 · Proximity — distance is meaning

Put related things close together; push unrelated things apart. The eye reads closeness as "these belong together." So the gaps in a layout aren't empty — they're telling the viewer where one idea ends and the next begins. [CRAP · Saylor]

The diagnostic is a counting test: how many groups should there be, and how many does your eye actually see? If a schedule has three talks, your eye should land on three blocks — not nine equal lines.

The rule of thumb

Space between groups should be clearly bigger than space within a group. If the gaps are all equal, you have no groups — just a list.

2 · Alignment — nothing placed arbitrarily

Every element should line up with something else on the page. Shared edges create invisible lines that the eye feels even when it can't see them — and those lines are what make a layout read as intentional rather than scattered. [Presentation Zen]

Two working rules: pick one alignment and commit (usually left), and be wary of centring everything — centred text has a different, ragged edge on every line, so it never builds a strong line.

For the systems thinker

Think of alignment as snapping to a grid and proximity as clustering. Both replace arbitrary placement with a rule. You're not making aesthetic choices so much as removing degrees of freedom — and a layout with fewer unexplained positions reads as more controlled.

Practice · Procreate · ~10 min

A "fix the mess" drill — pure proximity + alignment, no colour or font changes.

  1. New canvas. Type a six-line contact card: name, role, street, city, phone, email. Deliberately scatter it — centre some lines, indent others, push one to the right. Make it look like the "before" above.
  2. Duplicate the canvas. Fix it with two moves only: Alignment — drag every line to one shared left edge; Proximity — group name+role together, and street+city+phone+email together, with a clear gap between the two blocks.
  3. Compare. You changed nothing but position. Notice how much more "designed" the second one looks — that's the whole point.

Your win this lesson

You can now fix the most common "looks messy" problem with two free moves: group related items by Proximity (vary the gaps) and give everything one shared edge by Alignment. You also know the two diagnostics: count the groups, and find the shared edge.
