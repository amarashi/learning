Lesson 01 · The Map

Two layers, and everything sits on one of them

Before any technique, a map. Every piece of graphic design is made of just two kinds of thing — and most "I don't know why this looks bad" moments are a confusion between them.

Graphic design is arranging visual material to communicate — deciding what a viewer sees first, second, third, and how it all holds together. That's it. The whole field splits into two layers, and a trained eye keeps them separate.

Read the map top to bottom: you take elements (raw materials), you arrange them using principles (rules), and the payoff is communication — the viewer's eye goes where you intend. Design problems almost always live in layer ②: the materials are fine, the arranging is the issue.

For the systems thinker

Elements are data; principles are the transform; communication is the output. The same elements (a title, a date, a logo) run through different principles give wildly different results — exactly like the same dataframe through different pipelines. "Make it look better" is rarely about swapping the data; it's choosing a better transform.

Start with four: C.R.A.P.

The principles list looks long, but four of them do most of the heavy lifting and are enough to diagnose almost any amateur layout. Robin Williams packaged them with a memorable (rude) acronym in The Non-Designer's Design Book: Contrast, Repetition, Alignment, Proximity. [CRAP, Saylor]

  • Contrast — if two things are different, make them very different. Half-differences look like mistakes.
  • Repetition — reuse a colour / font / spacing so the piece reads as one coherent thing.
  • Alignment — nothing is placed arbitrarily; every edge lines up with something.
  • Proximity — related items sit close; unrelated items get space. Distance = relationship.

You don't need to memorise these. You need to be able to point at a design and name which one is missing. That's the whole skill of the trained eye — and it's what the next few lessons drill. Full definitions live in your glossary.

Practice · Procreate · ~10 min

Make a tiny event flyer twice — same elements, different principles. You'll see the map work.

  1. New canvas. With the Text tool, type six lines: event name, tagline, date, time, venue, price. Make them all the same size, evenly spaced. This is your "before."
  2. Duplicate the canvas. Now apply just two principles: Proximity — push the date/time/venue lines together as one block, with a gap before and after; and Contrast — make the event name much bigger and bolder than everything else.
  3. Put them side by side. Nothing was added — same words. Note what changed in how your eye moves across the second one.

Bring the two versions (or just what you noticed) to our next session — that's where it sticks.

Your win this lesson

You can now split any design into elements (the raw materials) and principles (how they're arranged) — and you know that "looks bad" usually means a principle is missing, not an element. You've met the four that matter most: Contrast, Repetition, Alignment, Proximity.
