Contrast & Hierarchy — what the eye sees first

Proximity and alignment made things tidy. Contrast decides what's loud. Used on purpose, it builds hierarchy: a deliberate first, second, third for the eye. This is the biggest single step from "typed" to "designed."

One idea runs through this whole lesson: contrast is the tool; hierarchy is the goal. You turn up the difference between elements (contrast) so the viewer's eye lands in the order you chose.

1 · Contrast — if it's different, make it obvious

When two things aren't the same, make them very different — not slightly. Timid contrast reads as a mistake; the eye can't tell if it's meant to be the same or not. Bold contrast reads as a decision. [CRAP · Saylor]

The rule of thumb

"Don't be a wimp." If you're making something different — bigger, bolder, darker — push it far enough that no one could mistake it for an accident.

2 · Where contrast really lives: value, not colour

Here's the trap for someone who thinks in colour first. Contrast feels like a hue job — "make it blue, make it red." But the strongest contrast comes from value: how light or dark something is, independent of its colour. [glossary · Value] Two different hues at the same value barely separate. A dark thing on a light thing always does.

The diagnostic is the squint test: blur your eyes (or desaturate the image) until hue drops out. What survives is your value contrast — and that's what actually carries the hierarchy. Try it:

In colour both cards look fine. Squint, and the left one turns to mud — its two hues sit at nearly the same value, so nothing survives. The right one stays perfectly legible. Value is what survives the squint.

For the systems thinker

Think of a colour as a vector with three components — hue, saturation, value. Contrast can act on any of them, but the eye weights value most for "what's foreground vs background." Optimise value first; treat hue as a second-order adjustment, not the lever.

3 · Hierarchy — contrast with a plan

Contrast applied at random just makes noise. Hierarchy is contrast used deliberately to rank elements by importance, so the eye reads them first, second, third. [IxDF · Visual hierarchy] Before you style anything, decide the ranking. Then use size, value and weight to enforce it.

Practice · Procreate · ~12 min

Build a hierarchy, then prove it with the squint test.

  1. New canvas. Type the same four lines as the poster above: name, hook, details, action. First make them all one size and weight — the flat "before."
  2. Duplicate. Now rank them: make the name big and dark, the hook a clear accent, the details small and muted. Use size and value to do the work — resist reaching for colour first.
  3. Squint test it. In Procreate, drop a Hue/Saturation adjustment to zero saturation (greyscale), or just physically squint at the screen. Does your ranking still read 1-2-3 in pure grey? If it collapses, your contrast was living in hue, not value — fix it and squint again.

Your win this lesson

You can now build hierarchy on purpose: decide the 1-2-3, then use contrast — chiefly size and value, not hue — to enforce it. And you have a hard diagnostic that ignores taste: the squint test. If it survives grey, the contrast is real.
